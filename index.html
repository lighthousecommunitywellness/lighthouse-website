What's in the name

Light

Why not-for-profit

The structure is the ethic.

A commercial studio has to treat wellbeing as a product. We don't want to sell wellbeing — we want to remove the barriers to it. That's easier to say than to structure, so we built the structure to match the words.

Running as a not-for-profit means revenue is reinvested, not extracted. Every dollar donated — whether at class or online — funds the next class, the next venue hire, the next person who needed a mat and couldn't afford one.

  • 01No profit motive — surplus goes back into classes, venues, and access.
  • 02Eligible for community wellness and mental-health grants that fund our operating model.
  • 03Board-governed and transparent, with outcome tracking built in from day one.
  • 04Kept structurally separate from any commercial venture — clean, accountable, single-purpose.
About

The idea, the name, the intention.

Lighthouse Community Wellness started with a simple observation: the people who most need mindfulness and mental-health support are often the least able to afford it.

The through-line

"No barriers — especially financial — to mindfulness and mental-health support."

The idea

There is no shortage of yoga on the Sunshine Coast. There are studios, teachers, retreats, trainings, merchandise, memberships. There is also a quieter truth: cost is the single most common reason people never walk into a class. Not scepticism about the practice. Not the yoga-body myth. Not time. Money.

Lighthouse Community Wellness exists to remove that barrier. A class every fortnight to begin with, donation-based, run from a hired community hall. If you can contribute, you do. If you can't, you come anyway. The people who can afford more effectively subsidise the people who can't — and nobody at the door knows who's who. That's the whole point.

The practices themselves — gentle yoga, breathwork, mindfulness — are offered not as a wellness product but as support for the ordinary human experience of having a hard season. Grief. Anxiety. Burnout. Loneliness. The space is designed to welcome people in those seasons, not impress people performing out of them.

The name

Each word earns its place.

Light does two things at once. A lighthouse exists to guide people through storms — to be a steady, visible, reliable presence in the dark. That's the outward meaning. But there's also the felt sense of lightness that follows a yoga or mindfulness session — less weight, less heaviness, more room to breathe. Both meanings belong.

House is not an afterthought. The body is the house the self lives in. You are your own home. The name points inward as much as outward: you are guided, and you are also — already — home.

Put together: a lighthouse is a beacon and a home at the same time. An outward light for people in dark seasons, and an inward reminder that the home they're looking for is something they already carry.

The NFP decision

Structure shapes behaviour. A commercial studio — however well-intentioned — is pulled by the gravity of revenue, margins, retention metrics, and growth. None of those things are wrong. They're just the wrong tools for this job.

Running as a not-for-profit is a deliberate structural choice to align incentives with the mission. There are no shareholders. There is no profit extraction. Any surplus is reinvested into more classes, more community reach, and the eventual capacity to hire an events coordinator so the programme can grow. The NFP form also makes us eligible for community wellness and mental-health grants — which, alongside donations, is how the whole model stays viable without charging people at the door.

NFP doesn't mean unpaid or unprofessional. It means no-one profits from the practice. The teaching is paid. The venue is paid. The administration is done properly. What's removed is the pressure to turn wellbeing into a product — and the barrier that creates for the people who most need support.

Where we are now

We're early — intentionally. Fortnightly classes to begin with, a Sunshine Coast venue being confirmed, and a small and growing group of regulars. The plan is to grow the way trustworthy things grow: slowly, transparently, and only as the funding and community support allow. Debt-free by principle. Grant-funded where it fits. Donation-sustained at the core.

If that sounds like something you'd like to be part of — as a participant, a supporter, or someone who simply wants to see more of this kind of thing in the world — there's a mat for you.

Other ways to help

If giving isn't the right fit right now.

Generosity isn't only financial. Presence, word-of-mouth, and a few hours of your time are how community initiatives like this survive their first year.

Donate your time

A few hours of your skills can change everything for us right now.

We're a young not-for-profit, and the things we need most aren't always money. Legal advice. Accounting and bookkeeping. Photography. Social media help. Setting up classes. Making a cup of tea for a nervous first-timer at the door. Helping pack mats away.

And the simplest things help too.

Come to a class. Bring a friend. Share our page with someone who might need it. Tell a local community group about us. Sometimes that's all it takes to reach the person we're meant to find next.

In the meantime

Small things that help, a little.

These don't replace professional support. They're small, gentle first moves for the in-between moments — the ones where you're not in crisis but you're not okay either.

Breathe slowly

Four seconds in. Six seconds out. Repeat for two minutes. A longer exhale signals safety to your nervous system. It's biology, not mysticism.

Tell one person

Not everyone. Not the internet. One trusted person — "I'm not doing well right now." That's enough. You don't have to have a plan or an ask.

Move, gently

A ten-minute walk. Slow stretches on the floor. Wash your face with cold water. The body calms the mind more reliably than the mind calms the mind.

Book a GP appointment

Your GP can write a Mental Health Treatment Plan — this gives you subsidised sessions with a psychologist through Medicare. It's the most underused first step.

Put down the phone

Scrolling while struggling is the emotional equivalent of drinking salt water. Aeroplane mode for twenty minutes. The world will still be there.

Come to a class

When you're ready. No commitment, no cost you can't afford. Sometimes just being in the same room as other people breathing is the whole intervention.
